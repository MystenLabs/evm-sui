# IPFS in 2026: a stack builders use, complain about, and quietly route around

**The dominant builder sentiment around IPFS in mid-2026 is pragmatic exhaustion, not enthusiasm.** Active EVM developers still ship with it — Uniswap, MetaMask, Aave, Snapshot, and most NFT projects depend on CIDs somewhere in their stack — but the conversation has shifted from "decentralized future" to "which paid pinning service is least likely to break my app this quarter." A brutal 75-day window in 2024 (Cloudflare gateway sunset, NFT.Storage Classic decommissioned, web3.storage rebranded to Storacha) shattered trust in the free-public-goods model that originally made IPFS attractive. The 2025 Shipyard maintenance push fixed many genuine performance gaps in Kubo, but the JS/dapp client surface remains thin, IPNS is universally treated as broken, and competitors — Walrus, Arweave/Irys, Akave, Filebase — are visibly peeling off use cases. A new EVM builder in 2026 asking around hears: "Use it because the standard demands CIDs and ENS contenthash works with it, but pay Pinata or Filebase, don't run a node, never touch IPNS, and budget for migrating in two years."

## The 2024 trust shock still defines the conversation

Three structural events landed in the same six-month window and remain the reference points for every storage discussion today. **Cloudflare's public IPFS gateway (`cf-ipfs.com`) was fully decommissioned on August 14, 2024**, after Cloudflare framed the exit as supporting the new Interplanetary Shipyard collective — though TorrentFreak documented that DMCA takedowns on the gateway had jumped roughly 10× to 10,139 actions in H1 2023, which builders widely interpreted as the real driver. **NFT.Storage Classic stopped accepting uploads at 5 PM EST on June 30, 2024**, affecting a stated 165,000 users and 660 TB of data, with the official warning that "over time, latency and availability may degrade." **The old `web3.storage` API was sunset January 9, 2024**, and the team rebranded to Storacha with a UCAN-based auth model that broke nearly every existing integration.

The cumulative effect was that Uniswap's interface release notes broke (GitHub issue Uniswap/interface#7753: "All references to Cloudflare's IPFS gateway should be updated because all these links will be broken soon"), MetaMask Mobile shipped a forced migration of stored gateway URLs (issue #11692), and small NFT projects spent months chasing dead metadata. The MiningClub.app team summarized the broader mood on Medium: "Web3 is amazing and full of limitless potential, but it's just not ready to serve static assets to a Web2 world today." That post is still recirculated in 2026 every time a public gateway hiccups.

## Gateways: from utilities to paid choke points

**Public gateways are now universally treated as unreliable**, and the conversation has fully shifted to "which paid gateway endpoint do you use." On the IPFS discussion forums (discuss.ipfs.tech/t/ipfs-gateway-slow/10736), a representative production complaint reads: "ipfs.io … 85–90% of the time, it resolves … sometimes takes 5 min" with a self-hosted gateway "98% of the time, it never resolves … returns 504 timed out." Boxo issue #862 from February 2025 surfaced a more troubling pattern from Shipyard maintainer Marcin "lidel" Rataj himself: "defunct gateways start returning redirects back to ipfs.io and dweb.link, effectively acting as amplification vector." In Kubo 0.38–0.39 the team had to introduce a `Gateway.RetrievalTimeout` (default 30s) explicitly because "configurable limits protect gateway resources during high load" — i.e., the public gateways were being abused.

The most-cited builder moment of the year was Vitalik Buterin publicly warning users away from `eth.limo` during an April 18, 2025 DNS registrar hijack, and routing them to a raw IPFS gateway URL as the safe fallback. That post was widely framed in replies as "this is exactly what IPFS is supposed to be good for" — a rare positive marquee event in an otherwise frustrated year, though it also underscored that the *DNS layer* over IPFS is itself a single point of failure.

Sentiment converges on three practical responses: pay Pinata or Filebase for dedicated gateways, self-host a Kubo node with the new AutoTLS feature shipped in late 2024, or just stop pretending and serve from S3 with the CID stored on-chain.

## The Pinata SDK treadmill and pricing fatigue

Pinata emerged from 2024 as the de-facto winner — OpenSea, Magic Eden, Sorare, Ledger, and Fileverse all run on it — but builder annoyance with Pinata's velocity of API changes is loud. **The canonical Pinata SDK has been replaced twice in roughly twelve months**: `@pinata/sdk` was archived in August 2024 with four unanswered 2024 issues left in place, then `pinata-web3` was deprecated in favor of a new `pinata` SDK whose API surface changed again (e.g., `pinata.upload.file()` → `pinata.upload.public.file()`). Older issues like Pinata-SDK#79 ("Exceeding the number of allowed requests per minute results in a 429 … time consuming to re-write") capture the recurring rate-limit complaint, and the IPFS Desktop bug tracker (ipfs-desktop#1954) documents Pinata rate-limiting even legitimate `/pin/remote service/ls --stat` health checks.

On pricing, Pinata's September 2024 blog post launching a more generous free plan is itself the strongest evidence of complaint pressure: "our Free Plan was too restrictive … so we changed that." But the deeper grievance, captured by Jim Dee in a frequently-shared Medium piece, is structural: **"Pinata is NOT a Viable Long-Term Solution for Facilitating Decentralized NFT File Storage on IPFS … my current preference for long-term NFT storage is Arweave."** Builders increasingly read Pinata's 2024 launch of a Files API for *private* S3-style storage, and its 2026 x402 USDC payment integration, as Pinata itself quietly conceding that pure public IPFS isn't a complete product.

## Storacha and the UCAN tax

The web3.storage → Storacha transition is the single most concrete grievance from EVM dapp builders who had Kubo-style workflows. Storacha replaced API keys with a UCAN/`did:key` capability chain, and its own FAQ openly states: **"To keep costs low, we no longer offer an implementation of the Pinning Service API."** That sentence broke the standard IPFS pinning integrations in every dapp toolchain that relied on it. The Storacha team's own January 2026 debugging guide concedes the new UX reality: "Failures are rarely about network reach; they are about proofs, capabilities, and context."

The loudest representative quote lives in Storacha GitHub issue w3up#1585, opened November 2024 and still open: **"This project's DX keeps going down as it ages like milk. Why CAN'T you keep using simple API keys and secret schemes as in the past? Anyhow, help me fix this. Otherwise, I rather move my entire IPFS infra to AWS S3."** Other issues echo the same frustration — w3up#1209 asking why CJS support was dropped from a package that worked fine in `web3.storage`, w3infra#134 documenting confusing UCAN error messages, and a steady backlog of unaddressed 2024–2025 issues (w3up#1612, #1621, #1634, #1644, #1645). A particularly alarming late-2025 thread on discuss.ipfs.tech reports: "storacha is eating up my money, and decided I want to cancel subscription. But there is no way to cancel starter subscription."

The freshest concrete worry surfaced in the official `ipfs-deploy` GitHub Action README, which now carries the line **"Storacha uploads will stop working on April 15, 2026. Please switch to an alternative provider."** That suggests a second wave of forced migration is either imminent or in progress as of report time and deserves verification — if true, it would mark the third disruptive transition for this product lineage in 30 months.

## NFT.Storage collapse fallout still echoes

Despite NFT.Storage Classic being officially decommissioned for uploads in June 2024, **the deprecated nft.storage GitHub repo continued receiving new issues nearly every week through 2025** — usernames like `Puppers2025`, `HankOfTheHill69`, and `49ergoldminers` filing complaints like "Its showing Error: storing files: API undergoing maintenance" suggest NFT project operators discovering broken metadata only when an end-user complained. Issue #2762 captures the refund frustration verbatim: "I want a refund. This is not the feature I want. It's very different from the old nftstorage. It doesn't work well."

NFT.Storage Ltd itself wound down in 2025, splitting products between Storacha (Classic continuity) and Lighthouse (long-term storage). The company's own November 2024 fundraising letter — "We need to raise $750,000 by November 10th to continue evolving towards a sustainable model" — and its disclosure of only $2,272 in April 2024 revenue made clear the free public-good model was structurally insolvent. **The lesson builders openly cite is "don't trust free decentralized storage to outlive your project."** Bankless's Metaversal newsletter, which is widely read by NFT-adjacent founders, now routinely frames IPFS as "data must be pinned through services like ClubNFT or Pinata to be maintained over time," and steers archival-grade projects toward Arweave or Walrus.

## Helia, Kubo, and the JS ecosystem gap

The official line, repeated across the IPFS blog and Helia FAQ, is unambiguous: **"js-IPFS is deprecated and will receive no further updates outside of emergency security fixes."** The migration to Helia, however, is genuinely painful, and the discontent is mostly about ergonomics, not philosophy. The most-cited builder writeup, by Jeremy Orme on Medium, is blunt: "How exactly you upload this data to the network using Helia is much more complicated than it was by calling a simple add() function in js-ipfs … we need to create a filesystem object and an encoder object manually … by using Uint8 iterables and asynchronous for loops 💀."

The technical debt shows up as a steady stream of issues: Helia#180 still requests remote pinning support that js-ipfs had; Helia#420 documents that a CAR-fetch example "spins up over 200 connections despite not needing the network at all"; Helia#164 reports an infinite WebTransport warning loop in Brave; Helia#657 admits the maintainers themselves discovered users "were unaware that Helia does not provide content by default. This is a different experience than Kubo, and we have no documentation mentioning this requirement." Bundle size is its own complaint — `@helia/utils` alone is around 859 kB minified+gzipped, prompting Helia#289 to propose a stripped-down `@helia/http` package "without bitswap/libp2p deps."

The strategic pivot inside the IPFS team itself — toward `@helia/verified-fetch` and HTTP-based verified retrieval rather than browser P2P — is widely read as **a tacit admission that pure peer-to-peer in browsers was impractical for dapps.** Pinata's own comparison post acknowledges the documentation gap: "Helia is still a newer implementation … the documentation is not quite as extensive as implementations like Kubo … you may need to dig into the Helia source code a bit to answer your questions." Confusion is compounded by users being told to migrate to Helia when the correct successor for many use cases is actually `kubo-rpc-client`, a complaint flagged on the IPFS forums and unresolved as of 2026.

Counterpoint: **Kubo itself is the bright spot of the entire ecosystem.** Under Shipyard maintenance, releases 0.34 through 0.41 in 2025–2026 delivered concrete wins — Bitswap broadcast reduction of "80–98% reduction in Bitswap broadcast messages, 50–95% savings in network bandwidth" (v0.36), DHT Sweep Provider by default (v0.39), automatic UPnP recovery, AutoTLS, embedded repo migrations, and a dramatic 24-minute → 30-second improvement in adding 10 GiB content. Shipyard's January 2026 retrospective opens with "2025 was the year IPFS became practical for regular users" — which is both a real achievement and an implicit admission about the prior baseline.

## IPNS is the most loudly broken primitive

Across every platform — GitHub, IPFS forums, Hacker News, even vendor blog posts — **IPNS is consistently described as too slow and unreliable for any user-facing workflow.** Pinata's own VP of Product wrote on the company blog in January 2025: "IPNS … has also been known to be slow and hard to work with. One of the biggest problems with IPNS is the speed." Pinata is now openly pitching its smart-contract-based IPCM (IPFS CID Mapping) on EVM L2s like Base as a *replacement* for IPNS — a remarkable position from inside the IPFS commercial ecosystem.

The technical evidence backs the perception. Helia issue #230 ("IPNS is not working consistently") remains open with the maintainer label `need/maintainers-input` and a representative quote: "subsequent new records … does not get resolved to at all instead each resolution returns the 1st published record only." Kubo#3860 has 31+ thumbs-up reactions ("first loading is very slow … reload after few minutes, it's again load slow"). The Kubo 0.34 release notes confirm the issue's scale: **"Many complaints about IPNS being slow are tied to the default --ttl in ipfs name publish, which was set to 1 hour. To address this, we've lowered the default IPNS Record TTL during publishing to 5 minutes."** Henry Wu, a personal-blog builder using ENS + IPFS, captured the chronic effect: "I have been subscribed to Vitalik Buterin's website for some time, and the RSS feed has been broken since I subscribed." Neiman's blog ("Why I Moved My Blog from IPFS to a Server") reached the Hacker News front page in early 2024 partly on the strength of: "IPFS doesn't include yet an easy command to always pin the latest content of IPNS … It just doesn't fit a personal blog needs yet."

## Providing, retrieval, and the "random walk" problem

The most candid official admission of structural performance problems lives in Shipyard's 2025 roadmap (ipshipyard/roadmaps#6): **"There should be no more issues where finding content ever takes more than 5s. When this happens today it is because data is not advertised into a mainnet content routing system by those hosting it resulting in kubo doing effectively a 'random walk' until it finds them."** Kubo issue #9982 quantifies the underlying decay: ">70% of provider records point to peers that are not reachable." Kubo v0.39's release notes are equally telling — "root CIDs typically become discoverable on the network in under a second (compared to 30+ seconds previously)," which means the prior baseline most builders experienced was thirty-plus seconds for fresh content visibility.

Garbage collection is the second performance landmine: Kubo#8870 hypothesizes (with evidence) that "request times … after a garbage collection show a tenfold increase in time to first byte," and #7008/#9553/#6878 catalogue multiple cases where data added via MFS or with `--pin=false` is silently deleted in ways developers found "surprising." Kubo#7752 (a meta-issue) bluntly references the situation: "IPFS Repo GC Is Unrealistic At Non-Trivial Scale: rm -rf + resync is faster."

## Self-hosting reality and Filecoin's complexity barrier

Running a Kubo node is technically possible but the hardware bar quietly climbed. The Kubo README now warns: "we recommend at least 6 GB of RAM and 2 CPU cores … Larger pinsets require additional memory, with an estimated ~1 GiB of RAM per 20 million items for reproviding to the Amino DHT … Running Kubo on underprovisioned hardware is at your own risk." Pinning operator complaints (Kubo#10596 from a 16M-pin operator: pin listing stopped responding for 16 hours; #10513 reporting that "executing 'ipfs bitswap reprovide', even for 20mb (about 200 files), is too much, and will systematically crash your ipfs daemon even with a quad core with 8Gb of ram") show that running anything resembling a production pinning service is non-trivial. UPnP regressions weren't fixed until Kubo v0.39 in late 2025.

Filecoin-as-IPFS-persistence remains widely considered too complex for "just storage" use cases. Filecoin-project/specs#1191 captures the founding confusion that has never fully resolved: "IPFS's caching facilities should limit load on the miner itself for heavily requested files … However after looking at the site it looks like you have to pay to retrieve a file off of a miner. These two things seem at odds with one another." The Tusky comparison piece, while vendor-aligned to Walrus, accurately describes the practical issue: **"files under 4 GiB are considered 'small-scale storage' and are much harder to store directly, as providers prefer large, high-efficiency storage deals."** The 2025 Walrus paper (Danezis et al., arXiv 2505.05370) cites Filecoin sealing latencies "on the order of 1.5 hours" — an order-of-magnitude mismatch with dapp metadata needs. Filecoin Saturn, the CDN layer that was supposed to bridge this gap, was folded into Storacha on June 1, 2024.

## "Decentralization theater": one Pinata gateway under everyone

The most enduring meme is structural, not technical. A widely-shared 2024 piece by Sohail Saifi captures it: **"Your ownership is permanent and verifiable, but your ability to actually access what you own depends on services that could disappear tomorrow … OpenSea actually improved their NFT metadata reliability by 99.2% when they switched to Pinata's infrastructure."** The peer-reviewed paper "The Cloud Strikes Back: Investigating the Decentralization of IPFS" (Balduf et al., arXiv 2309.16203) found IPFS retrieval is in practice dominated by centralized infrastructure — and that paper now gets cited reflexively in 2024–2026 builder threads about NFT centralization.

Three quotable summaries anchor the broader skepticism:
- **fiatjaf's "How IPFS is broken"** essay, still re-shared whenever a gateway breaks: "It's limited, slow, much slower than normal content-addressing fetches, most of the times it doesn't even work after hours … 'libp2p', the IPFS library for connecting people, is broken and is rewritten every 6 months."
- **vinta.ws's title meme**: "InterPlanetary File System … you could simply consider it as a distributed, permanent, but ridiculously slow, not properly functioning version of web." This phrase is recirculated almost monthly.
- **The Hacker News "Is anyone using IPFS in production?" thread (July 2024)**, where the top reply named only Library Genesis as a confirmed production user — and the thread itself drew only 6 comments, which commenters noted as its own answer.

Inside the IPFS team, the post-Bybit-hack blog post by Marcin Rataz ("Could IPFS prevent the Bybit hack?") essentially conceded the gap by arguing that *if* dapps used IPFS with local verification, supply-chain attacks like the AWS S3 frontend swap on Safe could have been prevented — which builders read as "they admit dapps still mostly host on S3."

## Where builders are quietly migrating

The pattern in 2024–2026 is not wholesale flight from IPFS but visible erosion at the edges, with three named alternatives capturing specific workloads:

- **Walrus (Mysten Labs / Sui)** launched testnet October 2024, raised $140M in March 2025, and went mainnet March 27, 2025. The headline migration is **Humanity Protocol moving "millions of credentials" from IPFS to Walrus**, with founder Terence Kwok stating "Walrus gives us the scale and performance needed to support the world's largest companies." Decrypt migrated its journalism archive, TradePort migrated NFT metadata, and Tusky (formerly Akord) migrated *from Arweave* to Walrus. The known caveats are a ~5× storage overhead from Red Stuff erasure coding, Sui-coupling (a January 14, 2026 Sui outage made Walrus writes unavailable while reads stayed up), and limited EVM-native uptake so far.
- **Arweave / Irys (formerly Bundlr)** continue to be the named "permanent storage" alternative for NFT archival and journalism. Irys rebranded in October 2023, raised $20M across 2024–2025, and launched IrysVM (EVM-compatible) with TGE in November 2025. Jim Dee's repeatedly-shared piece — "my current preference for long-term NFT storage is Arweave" — captures the migration thesis: pay once for permanence rather than rent-a-pin forever.
- **Akave** is the breakout S3-compatible enterprise winner of 2025–2026. **Akave Cloud launched July 7, 2025 as the first decentralized object storage on a dedicated Avalanche L1, claims "up to 80% cost savings vs AWS S3" with zero egress fees**, integrated with Snowflake in August 2025, and raised $6.65M in March 2026. Customers include The Defiant, Intuizi, Heurist.ai, and 375ai.

A second tier of practical migration involves **hosted IPFS providers** that preserve CIDs but absorb the operational pain: Filebase (S3-compatible, geo-redundant, 3× replication, defended its free tier in 2024 explicitly to contrast with NFT.Storage/web3.storage), 4Everland (used by Snapshot governance for Aave and Uniswap DAO votes), and Lighthouse (pay-once permanence model, official NFT.Storage long-term partner). And then the most candid quote of all comes from **Farcaster co-founder Varun Srinivasan on Warpcast**: "If you tried to use a DA to be the primary store of Farcaster data, you'd get very large disk size, slow replication and high costs … But honestly, **S3 might be 'good enough' for this use case**." That cast — a Farcaster co-founder publicly saying S3 might beat decentralized storage for a flagship social protocol — has been recirculated more than any single IPFS-skeptical post by an outside critic.

Codex (Status/Logos), originally one of the most-anticipated IPFS competitors, pivoted in August 2025 to align with the broader Logos stack and effectively reset its mainnet timeline — removing it from the near-term map.

## Positive counterpoints worth registering

The vibe is not unanimously negative, and three categories of happy users are worth surfacing. **First, Kubo itself is shipping at quality** — Shipyard's 2025 release cadence delivered concrete, measurable wins (80–98% Bitswap broadcast reduction, sub-second discovery, AutoTLS, AutoNAT v2) that genuinely change the operator experience. Second, **major EVM dapps actively endorse the IPFS + ENS contenthash pattern for frontend immutability**: Uniswap publicly documents this workflow, MetaMask uses it, and the IPFS team's "Could IPFS prevent the Bybit hack?" framing has gained traction with security-conscious teams considering verified retrieval for dapp frontends. Pinata's customer roster (OpenSea, Magic Eden, Sorare, Ledger, Fileverse) is a real signal that the production hot path works when you pay for it. Third, **niche builders genuinely love IPFS for archival and build-artifact use cases** where slow first-byte and pinning costs don't matter — Library Genesis remains the canonical production-scale example, and HN commenters cite using it for "fetching binaries by hash" outside Git as a clear win.

## The vibe a new EVM builder hears in 2026

Asked "should I use IPFS for my dapp?" by a 2026 newcomer, the composite answer assembled from builder discussions across Farcaster, GitHub, Reddit, HN, dev.to, and Mirror sounds like this: **yes, but only because the standards demand CIDs and ENS contenthash is non-negotiable**; pay Pinata or Filebase rather than trusting any free gateway; treat IPNS as broken and use a smart-contract CID pointer (Pinata's IPCM or your own mapping contract) instead; budget for a migration in two years because every "free public good" on this stack has been deprecated, rebranded, or repriced within 30 months; and if your project is media-heavy NFT or social, seriously evaluate Walrus, Arweave/Irys, Akave, or just S3-plus-CID before committing.

The deeper shift is that **the conversation has detached "content addressing" from "IPFS the network."** Builders increasingly want CIDs — for verifiability, for ENS, for on-chain references — without the obligation to participate in or trust the public IPFS network. That detachment is what Walrus is selling (CID-compatible blobs on Sui), what Akave is selling (S3 API with content addressing), what Pinata's Files API is selling (private content-addressed buckets), and what hybrid S3+CID stacks deliver in practice today. **IPFS in 2026 is less a decentralized network builders are excited about and more a set of interface conventions — CIDs, multihashes, IPLD — that survive even as the underlying P2P substrate gets routed around.** The conversation is no longer about whether IPFS won; it's about which of the post-IPFS designs inherit its useful primitives without inheriting its operational debt.
